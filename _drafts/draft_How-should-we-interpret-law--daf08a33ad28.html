How should we interpret law?


Should we interpret a law exactly how it is written? Or should we interpret it as it was intended to be? 

Let’s look at a small example concerning bananas, and I assure you, this is the most relevant a banana will ever be to a conversation about the nature of the philosophy of law, and even then, it’s tangential at best. 

Let’s look at what constitutes theft. As usual I’ll refer to the dictionary for my basis. 

Theft: “the action or crime of stealing.” 

That being, said; let’s define steal.

Steal: “to take (another person’s property) without permission or legal right and without intending to return it.”

This can actually be a bit of a slippery law to work with. Let’s look at some examples

Jim is a hypothetical person who gains a strong satisfaction from ordering bananas, using them for nefarious purposes, and then redistributing those bananas back into society by means of resale or otherwise. 

Suppose Jim goes into a grocery store, takes a banana without paying; and then simply brings it back later. Now what Jim intends to do with this banana is both irrelevant and probably not of significance to this post, but what we do know, is that he brought it back. 

Upon confronting Jim, he argues that he intended to return it the whole time? Did he steal the banana? Well, by definition, no. 

But this obviously isn’t what our law is intended to mean, right?! We know Jim took a banana without paying for it, (for some ridiculous use case) and simply returned it. Just because he returned it doesn’t mean he didn’t enjoy all of the perks and newfound pleasures in life that come with legal, banana ownership, that one would normally have to purchase a banana in order to partake in. 

But Jim’s gotten out of court scot-free because by law, he didn’t legally steal a banana. Some would argue that he actually did based on how we defined theft in the first place, but the law was imprecise. 

This is a problem that comes up a lot, when it comes to how we formalize our philosophical ideals, into laws that are realistic, that can be applied as analytically as possible. The reason to write laws this way, is because if a law is perfect, there is no disagreement about it’s meaning.

If it’s ambiguous, then it is up to the people reading the law, to interpret what it means, and if it applies. 

“It is up to the people to determine what a law means, and if it applies.” 

This is why we have judges, right? Most things that happen are actually very circumstantial, and it’s better to have a a human who can understand these circumstances to decide what the sentence that makes the most sense 

Well you might say, to make an assertion about the intention of a law, is a very subjective process and far more likely to be flawed than to analytically interpreting the laws exactly as they’re written. Which is a fine opinion to have. The only problem with this is that your laws would have to be adjusted constantly to adapt to many different circumstances and the possibilities are almost endless.
